# Punctuation and Grammar Rubric

## Overview
This rubric evaluates the correctness and effectiveness of punctuation and grammar usage in educational materials, ensuring clear and professional communication.

## Scoring Scale
- **4 - Exemplary**: Flawless grammar and punctuation enhancing readability
- **3 - Proficient**: Good grammar with minor errors not affecting clarity
- **2 - Developing**: Noticeable errors that occasionally impede understanding
- **1 - Inadequate**: Frequent errors significantly affecting comprehension

## Evaluation Criteria

### 1. Sentence Structure and Syntax (25%)

#### Exemplary (4)
- Perfect sentence construction throughout
- Excellent variety in sentence types and lengths
- Flawless parallel structure
- Sophisticated syntax enhancing meaning

#### Proficient (3)
- Generally well-constructed sentences
- Good sentence variety
- Mostly correct parallel structure
- Clear and effective syntax

#### Developing (2)
- Some awkward sentence constructions
- Limited sentence variety
- Occasional parallel structure errors
- Basic syntax with some confusion

#### Inadequate (1)
- Frequent sentence fragments or run-ons
- Monotonous sentence structure
- Regular parallel structure problems
- Confusing or incorrect syntax

### 2. Punctuation Accuracy (25%)

#### Exemplary (4)
- Perfect use of all punctuation marks
- Sophisticated punctuation for clarity and emphasis
- Flawless comma usage in complex sentences
- Perfect apostrophe and quotation mark usage

#### Proficient (3)
- Generally correct punctuation
- Good use of varied punctuation
- Most commas correctly placed
- Usually correct apostrophes and quotes

#### Developing (2)
- Some punctuation errors
- Basic punctuation only
- Inconsistent comma usage
- Occasional apostrophe/quote errors

#### Inadequate (1)
- Frequent punctuation errors
- Missing or misused punctuation
- Confusing comma placement
- Regular apostrophe/quote mistakes

### 3. Verb Usage and Agreement (25%)

#### Exemplary (4)
- Perfect subject-verb agreement
- Consistent and appropriate tense usage
- Excellent active/passive voice balance
- Sophisticated verb choices

#### Proficient (3)
- Generally correct agreement
- Mostly consistent tenses
- Good voice usage
- Appropriate verb selection

#### Developing (2)
- Occasional agreement errors
- Some tense inconsistencies
- Overuse of passive voice
- Basic verb choices

#### Inadequate (1)
- Frequent agreement errors
- Confused tense usage
- Poor voice control
- Limited or incorrect verbs

### 4. Pronoun and Modifier Usage (25%)

#### Exemplary (4)
- Perfect pronoun-antecedent agreement
- Clear pronoun references throughout
- Flawless modifier placement
- Excellent adjective/adverb usage

#### Proficient (3)
- Generally correct pronoun usage
- Usually clear references
- Good modifier placement
- Appropriate adjective/adverb use

#### Developing (2)
- Some pronoun agreement issues
- Occasional unclear references
- Some misplaced modifiers
- Basic modifier usage

#### Inadequate (1)
- Frequent pronoun errors
- Ambiguous references common
- Regular modifier problems
- Poor or incorrect modifier use

## Implementation Guidelines

### Assessment Process
1. Read through for overall grammar impression
2. Check systematic issues (agreement, tense)
3. Review punctuation usage
4. Evaluate modifier placement
5. Calculate weighted score

### Common Issues to Address
- **Comma splices**: Joining independent clauses with only comma
- **Dangling modifiers**: Modifiers without clear subjects
- **Pronoun ambiguity**: Unclear pronoun references
- **Tense shifting**: Inconsistent verb tenses within sections

### Improvement Recommendations
- Use grammar checking tools as first pass
- Read aloud to catch awkward constructions
- Create style sheet for consistency
- Implement peer editing process

## Common Error Reference

### Frequently Confused Elements

#### Punctuation
- **Its vs. It's**: Possessive vs. contraction
- **Affect vs. Effect**: Verb vs. noun (usually)
- **They're, Their, There**: Contraction, possessive, location
- **Your vs. You're**: Possessive vs. contraction

#### Grammar Structures
- **Who vs. Whom**: Subject vs. object
- **Which vs. That**: Non-restrictive vs. restrictive
- **Lie vs. Lay**: Intransitive vs. transitive
- **Fewer vs. Less**: Countable vs. uncountable

### Academic Writing Conventions
- Third person preference (varies by field)
- Present tense for general truths
- Past tense for completed research
- Avoiding contractions in formal writing
- Oxford comma usage (style-dependent)
